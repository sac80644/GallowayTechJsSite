What Is a Pattern?

So I don't always have the keenest mind when it comes to abstract thought. Unfortunately, that is often what patterns deal with. Let me clarify. I understand abstractions quite well, it is when you talk about these abstractions in conceptual terms that I have difficulty. I am a very concrete, hands on person and what really helps me is to see an idea. So one day, when I was in my backyard, it occurred to me. I had read recently that once is chance, twice is coincidence and three or more is a pattern. And at the time my wife was inside busily knitting. Oh, sorry hon, I meant crocheting.

Crocheting

Anyways, my wife told me how much she like the repetitive patterns used in her crocheting. Seeing a completed, or even partially completed piece of work, a light bulb when off in my head. I mean, I understood patterns, but now I could really see them. Each square of a quilt, all following the same pattern, then connected together to make a whole. I already knew that I used patterns all the time, I just did not realize that someone had written a book and gave them names.

Common Patterns

Early on in my career I did not think about patterns. For a while I just followed in the patterns that the more senior developers had chosen. After a while, these patterns became my go to, but I still had not read about patterns, nor did I know that the pattern that I was using had a name. Then I began to become more intentional in my approach. I started with "the simplest and most common patterns..."

Design Patterns : Elements of Reusable Object-Oriented Software

  • Abstract Factory
    • Intent - Provide an interface for creating families of related or dependent object without specifying their concrete classes.
    • Also Known As - Kit
  • Adapter
    • Intent - Convert the interface of a class into another interface clients expect. Adapter lets classes work together that couldn't otherwise because of incompatible interfaces.
    • Also Known As - Wrapper
  • Composite
      Intent - Compose objects into tree structures to represent part-whole hierarchies. Composite lets clients treat individual objects and compositions of objects uniformly.
  • Decorator
      Intent - Attach additional responsibilities to an object dynamically. Decorators provide a flexible alternative to subclassing for extending functionality.
      Also Known As - Wrapper
  • Factory Method
      Intent - Define an interface for creating an object, but let subclasses decide which class to instantiate. Factory Method lets a class defer instantiation to subclasses.
      Also Known As - Virtual Constructor
  • Observer
      Intent - Define a one-to-many dependency between objects so that when one object changes state, all its dependents are notified and updates automatically.
      Also Known As - Dependents, Publish-Subscribe
  • Strategy
      Intent - Define a family of algorithms, encapsulate each one, and make them interchangeable. Strategy lets the algorithm vary independently from clients that use it.
      Also Known As - Policy
  • Template Method
      Intent - Define the skeleton of an algorithm in an operation, deferring some steps to subclasses. Template Method lets subclasses redefine certain steps of an algorithm without changing the algorithm's structure.

Favorite Patterns

Below are just a few of my favorite patters with a brief reason:

Facade: So this pattern was probably the first that I can remember and the lead developer at the time referred to it as a Wrapper. This led to a bit of confusion on my part because there are two other patterns (Adapter and Decorator) that are also referred to as a Wrapper. But he was not referring to the same term. Just that it wrapped around a more complex process. The term does make sense.

Factory: There are many different types of factories. I have used this one a lot so it is one of my favorites.

Singleton: Simple and straightforward.

Observer: I first saw this one working on my first Federal contract. I haven't seen it implemented since but it stuck with me.
